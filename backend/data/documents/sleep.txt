Sleep and Recovery Guidelines

Definition:
- Sleep is a critical biological process that supports physical recovery, cognitive function, and overall health.

Recommended Duration:
- Adults should aim for 7–9 hours of sleep per night.
- Athletes and highly active individuals may benefit from 8–10 hours.
- Based on guidelines from the National Sleep Foundation and sleep research consensus.

Sleep Quality:
- Both duration and quality are important for recovery.
- Deep sleep (slow-wave sleep) plays a key role in muscle repair and hormone regulation.
- Poor sleep quality can impair recovery even if total sleep time is sufficient.

Performance and Recovery:
- Sleep supports muscle recovery, energy restoration, and adaptation to training.
- Inadequate sleep is associated with reduced strength, endurance, and reaction time.
- Chronic sleep deprivation increases injury risk.

Hormonal Effects:
- Sleep influences key hormones such as:
  - Growth hormone (muscle repair and recovery)
  - Testosterone (muscle development)
  - Cortisol (stress regulation)
- Poor sleep can disrupt hormonal balance.

Cognitive and Mental Health:
- Adequate sleep improves focus, decision-making, and mood.
- Sleep deprivation is linked to increased stress, anxiety, and reduced cognitive performance.

Sleep Hygiene (Best Practices):
- Maintain a consistent sleep schedule (same sleep and wake times daily).
- Avoid screens and blue light exposure before bedtime.
- Limit caffeine intake, especially in the afternoon and evening.
- Create a dark, cool, and quiet sleep environment.

Napping:
- Short naps (20–30 minutes) can improve alertness and recovery.
- Longer naps may interfere with nighttime sleep if taken late in the day.

Summary:
- 7–9 hours: general recommendation
- 8–10 hours: active individuals
- Sleep quality is as important as duration
- Poor sleep negatively impacts performance, recovery, and health

Evidence Base:
- National Sleep Foundation
- Sleep research consensus statements
- Peer-reviewed studies on sleep and athletic performance

Professional Guidance and Safety

When to Seek Professional Advice:
- If you consistently feel fatigued despite adequate sleep duration
- If you experience ongoing sleep disturbances (e.g., insomnia, frequent waking)
- If poor sleep is affecting daily performance, mood, or health
- If symptoms persist over an extended period

Important Note:
- While general sleep recommendations can improve recovery and well-being, individual health conditions may require professional evaluation.
- A qualified healthcare professional should be consulted for persistent or unusual symptoms.

Role of Guidance:
- General recommendations are intended for educational purposes
- They do not replace personalised medical advice, diagnosis, or treatment
