Protein Intake Guidelines

General Population:
- Adults require approximately 0.8 grams of protein per kilogram of body weight per day.
- Represents the minimum intake required to prevent deficiency.
- Based on global dietary guidelines from organizations such as the World Health Organization (WHO) and National Institutes of Health (NIH).

Active Individuals:
- Physically active individuals require higher protein intake to support recovery and adaptation.
- Recommended range: 1.2 to 2.0 grams per kilogram per day.
- Supported by the American College of Sports Medicine (ACSM) and International Society of Sports Nutrition (ISSN).

Muscle Growth (Hypertrophy):
- For increasing muscle mass, protein intake should typically range between 1.6 to 2.2 grams per kilogram per day.
- Higher intakes within this range may be beneficial during intense training or calorie deficits.
- Supported by systematic reviews in sports nutrition research.

Per Meal Recommendations:
- Consuming protein evenly throughout the day improves muscle protein synthesis.
- Optimal intake per meal: approximately 20–40 grams of high-quality protein.
- Supported by ISSN position stands.

Timing:
- Protein intake shortly after exercise supports muscle repair and recovery.
- A post-workout meal containing protein and carbohydrates is commonly recommended.

Protein Quality:
- High-quality proteins contain all essential amino acids (e.g., eggs, dairy, meat, fish).
- Plant-based diets can meet protein needs when a variety of sources are consumed.

Health Considerations:
- Higher protein diets are generally safe for healthy individuals.
- Adequate hydration is recommended when consuming higher protein levels.

Summary:
- 0.8 g/kg/day: minimum requirement (general health)
- 1.2–2.0 g/kg/day: active individuals
- 1.6–2.2 g/kg/day: muscle growth
- 20–40g per meal: optimal distribution

Evidence Base:
- World Health Organization (WHO)
- National Institutes of Health (NIH)
- American College of Sports Medicine (ACSM)
- International Society of Sports Nutrition (ISSN)
- Peer-reviewed systematic reviews in sports nutrition

Goal-Based Nutrition Planning

Weight Loss:
- Aim for a moderate calorie deficit
- Maintain higher protein intake to preserve muscle mass
- Focus on whole foods, fibre, and consistent meal timing

Maintenance:
- Match calorie intake to activity level
- Maintain balanced intake of protein, carbohydrates, and fats

Muscle Gain:
- Aim for a moderate calorie surplus
- Increase carbohydrate intake to support training performance
- Maintain sufficient protein intake for muscle growth

Meal Distribution

General Structure:
- 3 to 5 meals per day is commonly recommended
- Distribute protein evenly across meals
- Target 20–40g protein per meal

Example Daily Meal Structure:
- Breakfast:
  - Protein source (e.g., eggs, yogurt)
  - Carbohydrates (e.g., oats, toast)
- Lunch:
  - Lean protein (e.g., chicken, fish)
  - Carbohydrates (e.g., rice, potatoes)
  - Vegetables
- Dinner:
  - Protein source
  - Carbohydrates
  - Vegetables
- Snacks:
  - Protein-based options (e.g., yogurt, nuts, protein shake)

Example Plans

Weight Loss Example:
- Higher protein, moderate carbs, controlled portion sizes
- Focus on satiety and nutrient-dense foods

Muscle Gain Example:
- Increased portion sizes
- Higher carbohydrate intake to support energy demands
- Frequent meals to meet calorie targets

Practical Recommendations:
- Adjust portion sizes based on body weight, goals, and activity level
- Prioritise protein intake first, then adjust carbohydrates and fats
- Stay consistent with meal timing and overall intake

Personalisation Guidance:
- Protein intake should be calculated based on body weight (g/kg)
- Training frequency and intensity influence overall calorie needs
- Individual responses vary, so adjustments may be needed over time

Professional Guidance:
- If progress stalls, energy levels remain low, or symptoms feel unusual, consult a qualified healthcare professional or dietitian
