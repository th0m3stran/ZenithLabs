Training and Exercise Principles

Definition:
- Physical training involves structured exercise to improve strength, endurance, mobility, and overall health.

Types of Training:
- Resistance Training:
  - Builds muscle strength and mass
  - Includes weightlifting and bodyweight exercises
- Cardiovascular Training:
  - Improves heart health and endurance
  - Includes running, cycling, and swimming
- Flexibility and Mobility:
  - Improves range of motion and reduces injury risk
  - Includes stretching and mobility exercises

Training Frequency:
- At least 150 minutes of moderate-intensity exercise per week or 75 minutes of vigorous activity
- Strength training at least 2 times per week targeting major muscle groups
- Based on WHO and ACSM guidelines

Progressive Overload:
- Gradually increasing weight, volume, or intensity is necessary for improvement
- Without progression, performance gains plateau

Recovery:
- Muscles grow and repair during rest, not during training
- Overtraining can lead to fatigue, reduced performance, and injury

Program Design:
- A balanced program includes strength, cardio, and mobility
- Training should align with individual goals

Consistency:
- Long-term consistency is more important than short-term intensity

Injury Prevention:
- Proper technique reduces injury risk
- Warm-ups and cool-downs are recommended

Adaptation:
- The body adapts to repeated stress over time
- Variation in training helps maintain progress

Muscle Group Targeting

Chest:
- Exercises:
  - Bench press
    - Primary: chest
    - Secondary: triceps, shoulders
  - Push-ups
    - Primary: chest
    - Secondary: shoulders, triceps
  - Chest fly
    - Primary: chest
- Focus: pushing movements

Back:
- Exercises:
  - Pull-ups / chin-ups
    - Primary: back (lats)
    - Secondary: biceps
  - Rows
    - Primary: upper back
    - Secondary: biceps
  - Lat pulldown
    - Primary: lats
- Focus: pulling movements

Shoulders:
- Exercises:
  - Overhead press
    - Primary: shoulders
    - Secondary: triceps
  - Lateral raises
    - Primary: side delts
  - Rear delt fly
    - Primary: rear shoulders

Legs:
- Exercises:
  - Squats
    - Primary: quadriceps
    - Secondary: glutes, hamstrings
  - Deadlifts
    - Primary: posterior chain (glutes, hamstrings)
    - Secondary: lower back
  - Lunges
    - Primary: legs
  - Leg press
    - Primary: quadriceps

Arms:
- Biceps:
  - Curls
    - Primary: biceps
- Triceps:
  - Dips
    - Primary: triceps
    - Secondary: chest
  - Tricep extensions
    - Primary: triceps

Core:
- Exercises:
  - Planks
    - Focus: stability
  - Crunches
    - Focus: abdominal contraction
  - Leg raises
    - Focus: lower core

Exercise Types

Compound Movements:
- Multi-joint exercises (e.g., squats, bench press, deadlifts)
- More efficient for strength and muscle growth

Isolation Movements:
- Target a single muscle group (e.g., curls, lateral raises)
- Useful for muscle-specific development

Goal-Based Training

Muscle Gain:
- Focus on resistance training with progressive overload
- Moderate to heavy weights

Fat Loss:
- Combine strength training with cardio
- Maintain a calorie deficit

Endurance:
- Focus on cardiovascular training
- Longer duration, lower intensity

Beginner Guidance:
- Start with full-body workouts 2–3 times per week
- Focus on form before increasing intensity

Practical Recommendations:
- Train each major muscle group at least 2 times per week
- Use compound movements as the foundation
- Add isolation exercises to target specific muscles
- Ensure adequate recovery between sessions

Summary:
- 150 min/week moderate activity or 75 min vigorous
- Strength training at least 2 times per week
- Progressive overload drives improvement
- Compound movements are most effective
- Training should align with goals

Evidence Base:
- World Health Organization (WHO)
- American College of Sports Medicine (ACSM)
- Exercise science research consensus
